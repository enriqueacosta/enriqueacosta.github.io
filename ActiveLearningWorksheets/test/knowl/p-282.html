  1. To find the annual growth rate, we need to find a formula which describes the population, \(P(t) \text{,}\) in terms of the initial population, \(a \text{,}\) and the annual growth factor, \(b \text{.}\) In this case, we know that \(a=11,\!000\) and \(P(3)={16} ,\!000 \text{.}\) But \(P(3)=ab^3=11,\!000b^3 \text{,}\) so

    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} {16000} \amp = 11000b^3 \\ b^3 \amp = \frac{{16000} }{11000} \\ b \amp = \left(\frac{{16} }{11}\right)^{\frac{1}{3}}\approx 1.13303 . \end{aligned} \end{equation*}

    Since \(b\) is the growth factor, we know that, each year, the population is about \({113.303} \%\) of what it had been the previous year, so it is growing at the rate of \({13.303} \%\) each year.

  2. To find the continuous growth rate, we need a formula of the form \(P(t)=ae^{kt}\) where \(P(t)\) is the population after \(t\) years, \(a\) is the initial population, and \(k\) is the rate we are trying to determine. We know that \(a=11,\!000\) and, in this case, that \(P(3)=11,\!000e^{3k}={16} ,\!000 \text{.}\) Therefore,

    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} e^{3k} \amp = \frac{{16000} }{11000} \\ \ln e^{3k} \amp = \ln\left(\frac{{16} }{11}\right) \\ 3k \amp = \ln\left(\frac{{16} }{11}\right)\qquad\text{ (because )} \\ k \amp = \frac13\ln\left(\frac{{16} }{11}\right)\approx 0.12490 . \end{aligned} \end{equation*}

    So our continuous annual growth rate is \({12.49} \% \text{.}\)

  3. The annual growth rate, \({13.303} \% \text{,}\) describes the actual percent increase in one year. The continuous annual growth rate, \({12.49} \% \text{,}\) describes the percentage increase of the population at any given instant, and so should be a smaller number.
