1. If \(P(t)\) is the investmentâ€™s value after \(t\) years, we have \(P(t)=P_{0}e^{{0.07} t} \text{.}\) We want to find \(t\) such that \(P(t)\) is six times its initial value, \(P_0 \text{.}\) Therefore, we need to solve:

    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} P(t) \amp = {6} P_0 \\ P_{0}e^{{0.07} t} \amp = {6} P_0 \\ e^{{0.07} t} \amp = {6} \\ \ln e^{{0.07} t} \amp = \ln {6} \\ {0.07} t \amp = \ln {6} \\ t \amp = (\ln {6} )/{0.07} \approx {25.597} \hbox{ years.} \end{aligned} \end{equation*}

    With continuous compounding, the investment should sextuple in about \(25.6\) years.

  2. If the interest is compounded only once a year, the formula we will use is \(P(t)=P_0b^t\) where \(b\) is the percent value of what the investment had been one year earlier. If it is earning \({7}\%\) interest compounded once a year, it is \(10{7}\%\) of what it had been the previous year, so our formula is \(P(t)=P_{0}({1.07})^{t}.\)

    Using this new formula, we will now solve

    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} P(t) \amp = {6} P_0 \\ P_0({1.07})^{t} \amp = {6} P_{0} \\ ({1.07})^{t} \amp = {6} \\ \log ({1.07})^t \amp = \log {6} \\ t \log {1.07} \amp = \log {6} \\ t \amp = \frac{\log {6} }{\log {1.07}}\approx {26.482} \hbox{ years.} \end{aligned} \end{equation*}

    So, compounding once a year, it will take about \(26.5\) years for the investment to sextuple.
