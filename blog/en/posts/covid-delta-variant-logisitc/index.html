Covid-19 Delta Variant % Share of Cases Grew Logistically

December 15, 2021
Tags: Covid-19 , Data , Logistic , Tikz

Today as part of my work with the Mathematics Consortium Working Group (Hughes-Hallett Calculus textbooks) I stumbled upon the surprising fact that in many countries the % share of Covid-19 cases that were Delta variant grew almost perfectly logistically from 0% to 100% during 2021.

The graph below, which started all of this, should come up if one follows this Our World In Data link. (Image downloaded from Our World in Data, open access under the Creative Commons BY license.)

The curves clearly look “logistic-like”, meaning sigmoid shaped and symmetric. The general logistic function is of the form $$f(t)=\frac{L}{1+e^{-k(t-a)}}$$ and its graphs look as follows (figures created by me with Tikz, I’ll post the code below!)

Now, not everything that looks sigmoid shaped will be modeled well by a logistic, and there are clever ways to check this related to the differential equation of logistic growth (see appendix below), but I have lately grown fond of checking if something is logistic by just plotting it and with sliders trying to fit a logistic curve through the data, or making a spreadsheet model in which I can teak the parameters to see the plot fit. So this is what I set out to do, and was completely baffled by what I found!

Logistic fit for UK delta variant % share

A really good model for the delta variant share in the UK, where $t=0$ is February 8, 2021 is given by

  • $L=1$ (Delta variant reached $100\%$ share in 2021 before Omicron hit).
  • $k=0.09$ (related to $9\%$ initial exponential growth rate in the % share).
  • $a=139$ (this is just a horizontal shift in the curve and has not mathematically important meaning – it is just the moment of the year in which the growth rate of the delta share was highest, in $\%$ share per day).

Concisely: $$ \begin{array}{c} \text{Share of delta variant}\cr \text{cases in the UK} \cr \text{$t$ days after Feb 8, 2021} \end{array} = \frac{1}{1+e^{-0.09(t-139)}} $$

You can play around with the logistic fit yourself if the desmos app loads below. If it does not load, just follow this link, or download the data and play for yourself in the spreadsheet I created

Other (fancier) ways to tell if something is logistic

Of course, just because something looks sigmoid shaped does not mean that a logistic function is a good fit. In fact, logistic growth is governed by the differential equation $$ \frac{dy}{dt}=ky\left(1-\frac{y}{L}\right) $$ which can also be written as: $$ \frac{y'}{y}=k(1-y/L) $$ and this says that the relative growth, $y'/y$, (% change in one unit of time) is decreasing linearly as a function of $y$ from $y'/y=k$ when $y=0$ (growing at the beginning exponentially at continuous rate $k$) to $y'/y=0$ when $y=L$. These two ways to see the differential equation indicate two ways to see if something is growing logistically:
